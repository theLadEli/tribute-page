About

Mark Twain, whose real name is Samuel Langhorne Clemens, was born on November 30, 1835, in Florida, Missouri. He is known as one of America's most cherished authors and humorists. Growing up along the Mississippi River, Twain was deeply influenced by the culture and people of the region, which would later become central themes in his writing.

Twain's most famous works, The Adventures of Tom Sawyer (1876) and Adventures of Huckleberry Finn (1884), are considered classics of American literature, celebrated for their vivid depictions of youth, adventure, and social commentary. His sharp wit and keen observations on human nature have earned him a lasting place in the literary world.

Beyond his novels, Twain was a prolific essayist, journalist, and lecturer, known for his satirical style and ability to address serious social issues with humour. He traveled widely and often drew upon his experiences in his writing, making his work both entertaining and thought-provoking.

Mark Twain passed away on April 21, 1910, but his legacy lives on through his enduring contributions to literature and his influence on writers around the world. His work continues to be read, studied, and loved by readers of all ages, making him a timeless figure in American culture.
